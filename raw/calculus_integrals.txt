Calculus Integrals Study Guide
=============================

Introduction
------------
In calculus, an integral is a mathematical object that can be interpreted as an area or a generalization of area. Integration is the process of finding integrals. Along with differentiation, integration is a fundamental, essential operation of calculus.

The two main types of integrals are:
1. Definite Integral: Computes the net signed area bounded by the graph of a function between two limits.
2. Indefinite Integral: Represents a family of functions (the antiderivative) whose derivative is the original function.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus
----------------------------------
The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus links differentiation and integration:
It states that if a function f is continuous on the interval [a, b], and F is an antiderivative of f, then:

    \int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

Crucially, this shows that integration is the inverse operation of differentiation (finding derivatives).

Leibniz Notation
----------------
Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz introduced the modern integration symbol \int in 1675. The symbol was adapted from the "long s" character (representing "summa", Latin for sum or total). Leibniz wrote the integral of a function f(x) as:

    \int f(x) dx

Basic Integration Rules
----------------------
1. Power Rule for Integration: For any real number n not equal to -1, the integral of x^n is (x^(n+1)) / (n+1) + C.
2. Constant Multiple Rule: The integral of c * f(x) is c * \int f(x) dx.
3. Sum Rule: The integral of f(x) + g(x) is \int f(x) dx + \int g(x) dx.

Key Entities
------------
- Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz: German mathematician, introduced the integration symbol \int and formulated the notation we use today.
- Isaac Newton: English mathematician and physicist, formulated his own version of integration ("fluxions" and fluents) to solve physical problems of motion and gravity.
