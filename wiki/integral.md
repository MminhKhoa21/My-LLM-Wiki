# The Integral

The **integral** is a fundamental concept in calculus representing the accumulation of quantities, geometrically interpreted as the area under a curve.

## Definite vs. Indefinite Integrals

1. **Definite Integral**: The net signed area bounded by a function's curve, the x-axis, and vertical boundary lines. It yields a single numerical value:
   \[\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx\]
2. **Indefinite Integral**: The family of functions (antiderivatives) whose derivative equals the integrand. It includes an arbitrary constant of integration \(C\):
   \[\int f(x) dx = F(x) + C\]

## The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus connects integration with differentiation. If \(f(x)\) is continuous on \([a, b]\), and \(F(x)\) is its antiderivative:

\[\int_{a}^{b} f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)\]

This theorem establishes that integration is the inverse operation of finding a [[derivative]].

## History and Notation

The integral symbol \(\int\) was introduced by [[gottfried_leibniz]], derived from the Latin word *summa* (sum). [[isaac_newton]] also formulated integration principles, referring to them as "fluents" in physical models.
